# Retention Strategy Report

## Project Overview

The objective of this analysis is to identify customer segments that require different retention approaches before the deployment of a machine learning churn prediction model. Customer segmentation was performed using a combination of Recency, Frequency and Monetary (RFM) analysis along with additional behavioural signals including support interactions, return behaviour, campaign engagement, category diversity, loyalty membership and discount dependency.

The resulting segments represent distinct customer behaviours and business opportunities. Rather than applying a single retention strategy to all customers, targeted interventions can improve customer lifetime value while utilizing marketing resources more efficiently.

---

# Segment 1: Champions

## Customer Behaviour

Champions represent the most valuable customer group in the business. These customers have purchased recently, purchase frequently and contribute significant revenue. Their engagement levels are consistently high and they demonstrate strong loyalty toward the brand.

Because these customers already have a positive relationship with the company, they are less likely to churn in the immediate future. However, losing even a small number of Champions can significantly impact revenue due to their high lifetime value.

## Recommended Retention Action

The company should focus on recognition rather than discounts. Personalized appreciation programs, VIP benefits, early access to new products, referral incentives and exclusive memberships should be prioritized.

Champions should feel valued rather than targeted with generic promotions.

## Expected Business Value

Retaining Champions preserves the highest customer lifetime value and encourages advocacy through referrals and positive word-of-mouth marketing.

---

# Segment 2: Loyal Customers

## Customer Behaviour

Loyal Customers purchase consistently and maintain strong engagement with the platform. Although they may not spend as much as Champions, they generate predictable recurring revenue and often form the backbone of long-term business growth.

These customers respond well to personalized experiences and product recommendations.

## Recommended Retention Action

The organization should implement loyalty point acceleration programs, personalized product recommendations and cross-selling initiatives. Reward programs that encourage higher spending can help move Loyal Customers into the Champion segment.

## Expected Business Value

Improved customer lifetime value, increased average order value and stronger customer retention.

---

# Segment 3: New Customers

## Customer Behaviour

New Customers have joined recently and have limited purchase history. Because the relationship is still developing, their future behaviour remains uncertain. Many customers churn during this early lifecycle stage if engagement is not maintained.

## Recommended Retention Action

A structured onboarding journey should be implemented. Welcome emails, product education campaigns and incentives for second purchases can help establish long-term purchasing habits.

The primary objective is to convert first-time buyers into repeat customers.

## Expected Business Value

Higher customer retention during the acquisition stage and improved conversion into long-term customers.

---

# Segment 4: At Risk Customers

## Customer Behaviour

At Risk Customers historically contributed meaningful revenue but have recently reduced their engagement. Their recency scores indicate declining activity despite a history of valuable interactions.

These customers are among the most important retention targets because they have already demonstrated willingness to spend.

## Recommended Retention Action

Personalized win-back campaigns should be deployed. Product recommendations based on historical purchases, special offers and reminder communications can encourage re-engagement.

The focus should be on restoring activity before complete churn occurs.

## Expected Business Value

Recovery of previously valuable customers at a lower acquisition cost than acquiring new customers.

---

# Segment 5: Dormant Customers

## Customer Behaviour

Dormant Customers have remained inactive for extended periods. Their engagement levels are extremely low and the probability of natural return is limited.

Although some customers can still be recovered, conversion rates are generally lower than other segments.

## Recommended Retention Action

Limited-budget reactivation campaigns should be implemented. Time-sensitive offers, survey-based outreach and personalized reminders may generate selective recoveries.

Because recovery probability is relatively low, marketing spend should remain controlled.

## Expected Business Value

Incremental recovery of lost customers without excessive retention expenditure.

---

# Segment 6: Discount Sensitive Customers

## Customer Behaviour

Discount Sensitive Customers demonstrate strong dependence on promotions and discounts. Purchasing behaviour increases significantly during campaigns but declines during regular pricing periods.

While these customers contribute revenue, excessive discounting can reduce profitability.

## Recommended Retention Action

Targeted coupon distribution and personalized promotional campaigns should be used instead of broad discounts. Dynamic pricing strategies can improve profitability while maintaining engagement.

## Expected Business Value

Retention of promotion-driven customers while controlling discount-related costs.

---

# Segment 7: High Value Unhappy Customers

## Customer Behaviour

High Value Unhappy Customers contribute substantial revenue but simultaneously exhibit warning signs such as elevated complaint rates, support interactions or product returns.

This segment presents one of the highest churn risks because dissatisfaction is already visible despite continued spending.

## Recommended Retention Action

Immediate intervention is recommended. Dedicated support teams, proactive outreach and issue-resolution programs should be prioritized.

Customer concerns must be addressed before dissatisfaction converts into churn.

## Expected Business Value

Protection of significant revenue streams and prevention of high-value customer loss.

---

# Segment 8: Regular Customers

## Customer Behaviour

Regular Customers demonstrate moderate activity levels across most behavioural dimensions. They are neither highly engaged nor at immediate churn risk.

Although individually less valuable than Champions or Loyal Customers, they represent a large proportion of the customer base.

## Recommended Retention Action

Product discovery campaigns, seasonal promotions and personalized recommendations should be used to increase engagement.

The objective is to move these customers into higher-value segments over time.

## Expected Business Value

Long-term growth of customer lifetime value through gradual engagement improvements.

---

# Campaign Budget Prioritization

Assuming a limited retention budget of ₹1,00,000, resources should be allocated according to expected return on investment.

| Priority | Segment | Budget Allocation |
|-----------|----------|------------------|
| 1 | High Value Unhappy | 30% |
| 2 | At Risk | 25% |
| 3 | Champions | 15% |
| 4 | Loyal Customers | 10% |
| 5 | New Customers | 8% |
| 6 | Discount Sensitive | 5% |
| 7 | Regular Customers | 4% |
| 8 | Dormant | 3% |

High Value Unhappy Customers receive the highest allocation because a small improvement in satisfaction can protect significant revenue. At Risk Customers receive the second-highest allocation because they have already demonstrated strong historical value. Dormant Customers receive the lowest allocation because recovery probability is comparatively limited.

---

# Conclusion

The analysis demonstrates that effective customer retention requires segment-specific interventions rather than a universal marketing approach. By combining RFM metrics with behavioural indicators, the company can identify distinct customer groups and allocate resources where the expected business impact is greatest. This approach improves retention efficiency, maximizes customer lifetime value and reduces unnecessary marketing expenditure.
